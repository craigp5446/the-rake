# The Rake
*A transparency project for tech revenue models and user alignment*

---

## The idea

Most people have no framework for understanding how the tech products they use actually make money and who those products are truly optimising for. An app can look helpful, feel generous, and still be structurally designed to work against the user's interests. Sometimes that's obvious, but much of the time it is obscured by design.

The Rake aims to be an accountability layer for tech business models. It scores companies on how they make money, who benefits, and how much pressure they're under to extract value from users rather than deliver it.

---

## The problem

Tech products rarely make their complete revenue model legible. Users sign up for a free tool without understanding they're the product. They subscribe to a service without knowing the company is under investor pressure to double revenue in 18 months. They engage with an app without realising the engagement itself is the product being sold to advertisers.

This information asymmetry benefits companies. The Rake exists to disclose it.

---

## The format

A newsletter. One company report at a time, run through a published scoring framework, with sources cited and methodology open.

Each issue covers one company in depth. Its revenue model, ownership structure, design decisions, and where its incentives align or conflict with the people using it. Everything is grounded in public information: funding records, pricing pages, terms of service, investor reports, published research. No speculation presented as fact. Conclusions are clearly framed as analysis, not accusation. See the source confidence section below for more detail on this.

The research and scoring is done systematically, using a three-phase process: an AI agent collects and organises public evidence, a second agent applies the scoring framework. A human reviews, checks sources, does the final analysis and writes the narrative. It is important to note that this final step is completed by a human, and not generated by an LLM (though potentially with some copy editing support).

The framework is open — you can read exactly how scores are produced.

---

## The scoring framework

Each company is rated across seven dimensions. Scores are not collapsed into a single number. The profile across dimensions is more revealing than any aggregate.

The seven dimensions are:

- Revenue clarity
- Incentive alignment
- Captivity
- Engagement extraction
- Multi-sided tension
- Algorithmic transparency
- Ownership pressure

Every profile also includes a label (one of five editorial categories from Adversarial to Principled) and a spectrum position (a 0–100 placement within that label). Two companies can share a label while sitting at meaningfully different positions.

Flags surface the most significant positive and negative findings from the research — specific documented incidents or structural commitments that are important enough to affect how a dimension score should be read. Every flag is tied to a specific dimension and requires a cited source. Flags come in two types: negative (a documented incident or structural problem) and positive (a commitment or action that genuinely distinguishes a company from the norm).

- **Adversarial** (0–20) — designed to work against you
- **Extractive** (21–40) — takes more value than it delivers
- **Compromised** (41–60) — genuine value but not fully on your side
- **Aligned** (61–80) — makes money when you get value
- **Principled** (81–100) — actively prioritises your interests, sometimes at cost to itself

Full definitions, rubrics, and scoring guidance are in the methodology document.

## Source confidence

Every source used to inform a score is classified as either Assessed or Inferred.

- **Assessed** — primary sources: company websites, official filings, terms of service, pricing pages, verified financial reports
- **Inferred** — secondary sources: journalism, logical deduction from business structure, community reports, reported but unverified claims

Each dimension shows a confidence split, detailing how many sources were assessed versus inferred, and the resulting confidence level (High, Medium, or Low). A company that is hard to score confidently is exhibiting opacity, which is itself worth surfacing.

Community and company verification are planned features for a future version of The Rake. For now, all scores are produced by the editorial process described above.

---

## The open methodology

The scoring framework, research prompts, and versioning history are all public and forkable. You can't build a transparency tool with an opaque methodology, so The Rake doesn't try to.

If you disagree with a score, the methodology is your starting point. Please suggest improvements to the methodology so we can produce even better reports.

If you provide new, reliable information that may impact a company's report, please share this source and we will consider running an update in an upcoming report. We will however keep the historical record preserved, unless we find that the system made significant errors.

## Who this is for

Anyone who's ever felt vaguely manipulated by a product and couldn't quite articulate why.

Anyone who cares about the digital products they use and pay for, and want to make informed decisions about where they direct their attention and money.

Anyone building a company that wants to evaluate their own revenue models and business practices.

And in the fullness of time: journalists who want a citable framework, regulators who want a consumer-facing translation of business model analysis, ethically-positioned companies who want a credible third-party signal, and researchers studying the downstream effects of misaligned tech incentives.

---

## What The Rake is not

- It is not a legal document or regulatory filing
- It is not a claim about what companies do in private
- It is not a campaign to get companies shut down
- It is not affiliated with any company, investor, or advocacy group

It is analysis, applied consistently, in public, with sources.
