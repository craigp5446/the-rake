### DIMENSION: Revenue Clarity

**SCORE:** 3 — Clear

**RATIONALE:** Substack's revenue model is stated plainly on its public-facing pages: the platform takes a 10% cut of paid subscription revenue, with writers keeping 90% minus credit card processing fees (2.9% + $0.30 per transaction plus 0.7% recurring billing fee). The company's about page explicitly states "we only make money when you do." The December 2025 native sponsorships pilot represents a secondary revenue stream that has been publicly announced, though it remains limited to select creators.

### DIMENSION: Incentive Alignment

**SCORE:** 3 — Aligned

**RATIONALE:** Substack's 10% revenue-share model structurally ties the company's income to creator success—Substack earns nothing from free newsletters and only profits when writers convert readers to paid subscriptions. The Notes algorithm is explicitly designed to optimize for subscriptions rather than time-on-platform, contrasting with ad-driven social media. The company makes more money when creators succeed at building paying audiences.

### DIMENSION: Captivity

**SCORE:** 3 — Portable

**RATIONALE:** Writers can export their full content and subscriber email lists at any time via self-serve export functionality, with comprehensive data including email addresses, subscription status, payment information, and engagement metrics in CSV format. Substack's documentation explicitly states writers own their content, subscriber list, and payment relationships. Reader subscription cancellation is self-serve on web with benefits continuing until billing period ends, and a pause option is available.

### DIMENSION: Engagement Extraction

**SCORE:** 2 — Present

**RATIONALE:** The Notes algorithm is designed to optimize for subscriptions rather than time-on-platform, and the platform explicitly contrasts its approach with ad-driven social media. However, engagement mechanics exist: algorithmic ranking in the Notes feed, "restacking" (similar to retweeting), and social signals that determine content distribution. The app now drives more than a third of all paid subscriptions, indicating the platform benefits from in-app engagement. Livestreaming with AI-generated highlight clips was added in 2024.

### DIMENSION: Multi-Sided Tension

**SCORE:** 2 — Mixed record

**RATIONALE:** Substack is primarily a two-sided marketplace (writers and readers), but the December 2025 native sponsorships pilot introduces advertisers as a third party. The company states creators choose and approve brand partnerships without editorial oversight. However, revenue concentration in top creators (90% from top 5%) creates structural tension where Substack may prioritize high-earning creators over average users. The advertising program is new and its effects on writer-reader dynamics remain untested.

### DIMENSION: Algorithmic Transparency

**SCORE:** 2 — Partially disclosed

**RATIONALE:** Substack's head of machine learning publicly explained the Notes algorithm's design goals and general approach—optimizing for subscriptions using user characteristics including location, language, subscriptions, followed creators, and stated interests. However, specific ranking signals and weights are not disclosed. The algorithm surfaces content to non-followers based on undisclosed "overlapping audience signals" and has changed multiple times, with users noting variability in what content gets boosted.

## PROFILE

**PROPOSED LABEL:** Aligned

**PROPOSED SPECTRUM POSITION:** 68

**REASONING:** Substack's revenue-share model structurally aligns company and creator interests—the platform only profits when writers succeed at building paid subscriber bases. Strong portability (score 3) and revenue clarity (score 3) reinforce this alignment. However, the introduction of advertising in December 2025 creates new multi-sided tensions, algorithmic transparency is only partial despite public statements, and VC ownership at $1.1 billion valuation creates trajectory risk. The product is meaningfully aligned with user interests today but shows early signals of the compromises that typically follow late-stage funding.

## FLAGS

**FLAG:** Ads introduced
**REASON:** Substack launched "native sponsorships" pilot in December 2025 after historically positioning itself as an alternative to advertising-based monetization, introducing a third-party revenue stream.
**SOURCE:** https://www.cityam.com/substack-implements-native-advertising-following-1-1bn-valuation/ | December 12, 2025

**FLAG:** Security breach
**REASON:** Substack disclosed in February 2026 that unauthorized access in October 2025 exposed email addresses, phone numbers, and internal metadata for approximately 697,000 users, with a four-month dwell time before detection.
**SOURCE:** https://techcrunch.com/2026/02/05/substack-confirms-data-breach-affecting-email-addresses-and-phone-numbers/ | February 5, 2026

**FLAG:** Policy violation
**REASON:** In November 2023-January 2024, Substack faced criticism for hosting Nazi and white supremacist content, ultimately removing five publications after a pressure campaign while maintaining its light-touch moderation stance.
**SOURCE:** https://www.nbcnews.com/tech/tech-news/substack-removed-newsletters-criticism-nazi-content-rcna132963 | January 9, 2024

## PERSONA TAKEAWAYS

**For writers/creators:** Substack's business model is genuinely aligned with your success—the company only makes money when you earn paid subscriptions—but the introduction of advertising and VC pressure at unicorn valuation suggest the relationship may shift over time.

**For readers:** Substack shows you content based on an algorithm optimized for subscriptions rather than ad impressions, but the platform doesn't fully disclose how that algorithm decides what you see beyond your explicit subscriptions.
